As our investigation concludes, one question remains unanswered: who is accountable when patient data disappears? The answer reveals deep structural problems in how the NHS governs its digital assets.

<!--more-->

## The Blame Game

When we approached NHS trusts for comment, we received a familiar pattern of responses. Each organisation pointed to someone else.

- Trust IT departments blamed national systems
- National bodies pointed to local implementation
- Contractors cited specification documents
- Everyone claimed to be following protocols

## Regulatory Gaps

The Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) has the power to investigate data breaches, but their scope is limited.

"We can investigate individual incidents," an ICO spokesperson told us. "But systemic failures across multiple organisations are harder to address."

### What the Regulators Found

When regulators did investigate, they found:

1. Inadequate data protection impact assessments
2. Poor documentation of data flows
3. Insufficient staff training
4. No clear chain of responsibility

## The Cost of Inaction

The financial cost of this failure runs into millions. But the real cost is measured in patient outcomes.

> "My mother's cancer screening results were in those lost records. By the time we reconstructed her history, we'd lost precious months."
> <cite>Affected Family Member</cite>

## What Needs to Change

After months of investigation, we've identified key reforms needed to prevent future failures:

### Immediate Actions

1. **Mandatory data reconciliation** after any system migration
2. **Independent audits** of NHS digital infrastructure
3. **Clear accountability frameworks** for data governance
4. **Protected status** for IT whistleblowers

### Long-term Reforms

1. Dedicated funding for legacy system replacement
2. Centralised data governance standards
3. Patient access to their own data
4. Regular stress-testing of backup systems

## The Response

Following our investigation, NHS England has issued a statement acknowledging the issues and committing to a review. Whether this leads to meaningful change remains to be seen.
